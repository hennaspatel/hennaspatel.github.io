The potential of internet voting 

Like any technological innovation, there will be issues. They will need to be addressed, re-addressed, figured out and fortified.  But there is a promising outlook for election voting on the internet, backed by blockchain technology. 


And the impact could be transformative.  

Blockchain brief.

The blockchain is an public registry of all transactions and ownerships by way of a decentralized database. It is immutable, transparent, permanent and entirely user-owned. Cryptography and digital signatures are used to prove true identity and allow users to engage in online transactions, and to obtain access to records. Have a look at this  2-minute video primer for fundamentals of blockchain technology from the Institute for the Future.

How does blockchain work for voting?

"Every time a change is made to the chain (in this case, someone new casts a ballot), the entire ledger is updated. Because the information is not being stored in a centralized location, it’s virtually impossible to hack. Mobile phone technology, like fingerprint and face recognition, helps ensure that the person casting the vote is who they say they are" (Montgomery, 2018).

This infographic from the organization Follow My Vote helps visualize the process. (Followmyvote.com, 2017)

OK! But are there any real world implentations?


Estonians as Digital Citizens: As expected, Estonia has been an early adopter of blockchain technology for several government registries including health, judicial and security. 

The Markets: Stock exchanges around the world are developing or already pilotiing blockchain implementations for shareholder voting. These include: NASDAQ, TMX, JPX, ASK and NSE among numerous others.  

NATO: Actively investigating blockchain applications for military logistics, procurement and financing (Basu, 2016).

2018 Sierra Leone Election:   Reportedly the first ever blockchain-powered national election took place in Sierra Leone, in March 2018.
However, subsequent reports have shown this to be inaccurate. And it is reputedly a case where an over-zealous Swiss blockchain technology company and the electoral commision of Sierra Leone were at odds: 

"Sierra Leone’s election officials say that’s (blockchain voting) not what happened. The National Electoral Commission (NEC) is the “sole authority” on Sierra Leone’s public elections, and the group has gone out of its way to make it clear that it did not use blockchain in the March 7 election" (Houser, 2018).
